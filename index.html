Rocketry Projects

My highschool offered a three year rocketry program under the System's Go curriculum in which students are tasked with researching, designing, and constructing three different high-powered rockets each with its own goal. Throughout the program in addition to the design process, we built mathmatical and CAD models in Excel, Google Sheets, Fusion 360, and Solidworks. These models were then presented to NASA, Boeing, and other experts to ensure the success of our designs. Below I have detailed the different projects and my roles. Tsiolkovsky Project
Description: The Tsiolkovsky or 1 pound/1 mile project, students must bring a 1lb payload to a alitude of one mile with a budget of 1,000 dollars
Results: Painted in red and black the rocket reached a final alitude of roughly 4000 feet and successfully recording the flight
Oberth Project
Description: The Oberth/Transonic level of the Systems Go curriculum tasks students with breaking the sounds barrier, remaining under a 13,000 feet ceiling, and maintaining a 900 dollar budget
Results: While not confirmed due to circumstances outside our control the results of the flight are currently a final alitude of greater then 10,000 feet and top speed of roughly mach 1.07
Goddard Project
Description: The program culminates with the Goddard level where students are tasked with designing a rocket capable of carring a 5lb payload to world-record breaking altitude of 50,000 feet with a given budget of 10,000 dollars
Results: World record breaking final alitude of 49,523 feet ASL and a top speed of mach 1.6

Research/Design Projects

Over the past few years I have been part of a couple research and design projects where I was able to apply my skills in propulsion systems, experimental design, data acquisition, CAD, and CNC while also gaining valuable real-world experience, expanding my current knowledge, and learning more about the forces that dictate amazing and fundamental phenomena.

Princeton EPPDyL
Description: In conjuction with NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory Princeton Electric Propulsion Lab is developing a Lithium-Lorentz Force thruster (A type of Magnetoplasmadynamic (MPD) thruster) with a power output of 30 kiloWatts in hopes to scale to power output to number upward of 250 kiloWatts.
Wing Project
Description: This was the final project for my engineering design class which culminated of material research and comparison, stress analysis, CAD, and milling (both manual and CNC). The goals of the project were to construct the lightest wing possible, with minimum span of 23 inches, while also minimizing delfection under a 90 pound load.
